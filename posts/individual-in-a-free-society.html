Summary

In his 34th Steinmetz Memorial Lecture, "The Individual in a Free Society," Vice Admiral H. G. Rickover articulates deep concerns about the erosion of individual freedom and democratic principles in modern America. He identifies several key threats, including the replacement of the Protestant Ethic with a distorted "Freudian Ethic" that diminishes personal responsibility, the unbridled influence of technology that dictates human behavior, and the disappearance of historical advantages like abundant land and work dignity that once fostered independent citizens. Rickover's central argument focuses on the rise of giant, bureaucratic organizations—both private and governmental—which he contends interpose themselves between the citizen and government, diminishing individual autonomy and political effectiveness. He draws upon historical figures and documents, citing the Declaration of Independence, the Constitution, and thinkers such as Jefferson, Madison, Eisenhower, and Freud, to emphasize the foundational importance of the autonomous individual, ultimately urging a reassertion of citizen control and critical engagement to safeguard a truly free society.

In recent years a sense of uneasiness has crept upon us. We have lost some of our exuberance, some of our faith in ourselves. Many Americans are disturbed by the loss of good things we cherish as perculiarly American, and by the intrusion of distasteful things we never expected would invade our way of life. If this brings home to us the fact that human values making for civilized life cannot be created or preserved without continuous effort, the present period of uncertainty will in the long run prove beneficial.

In a democracy such continuous effort must come from the people, or rather from the individual citizen. Self-government will not produce a good society unless enough citizens feel a sense of identification with the fate of the nation. Repeating patriotic cliches is not enough; good citizenship involves mental activity, as well as a sense of duty to keep our society under close observation to make certain that common practices will always conform to the basic tenents of democracy. One such tenent is respect for

the equal moral worth of all human beings and the equal right of all men to

be free to shape their lives as they see fit, provided only they harm no one

and violate no law.

Our nation was launched with a system of government containing numerous safeguards to protect individual liberties. But in the intervening years, . especially since the coming of the Industrial and later the Scientific Revolution, radical changes have occurred in the American way of life, some of them inimical to the autonomous individual in whom our society is grounded.

It is with these changes that I am concerned. A quick glance at our national

origins will help us understand them better.

At the time of the Revolution, the settled region along the Atlantic seaboard was on the whole still a faithful replica of England, and most of the colonists considered themselves Englishmen. The social profile differed in that both the top and the bottom levels were missing, but life was not noticeably more “democratic”; only one man in fourteen was considered a "gentleman." However, where the settled region ended and the wilderness began an entiely new kind of society had begun to evolve; a society quite unlike anything in the old world. It was the immense achievement of the Founding Fathers that they realized this new type of society, the new kind of man who developed on the frontier, the whole rich empty Continent at the backdoor of the older sottlements--these offered a unique eppartantty to establish here the sort ‘of Utopia that rationalist philosophers had Long oe dreaming about: where, ‘regardless of birth or wealth, all men were free and government was subject to their consent.

The Founding Fathers were men of the Enlghtenment, one of the germinal eras of Western civilization. They were steeped in the classics and in rationalist philosophy. Indeed they were political philosophers of the first rank, besides being eminently practical and skillful politicians. We have never had their equal since. They took ideas that Western civilization had painfully evolved over many centuries and expressed them in words of such force and splendor that these not only won the enthusiastic support of the colonists but have continued ever since to inspire men all over the world. The ideas that sum up our political philosophy are contained in our Magna Carta--the

Declaration of Independence. The political framework that gives reality to them

government derives its "just powers" from the governed. Clearly, the Founding

Fathers wanted to make certain that never would Americans be ruled by_ anyone who had not received their express mandate.

To set up a system of government subject to control of the people was an extraordinary thing; all the wise and "practical" men of Europe knew it could never work. I think we need to be quite clear in our minds that all this is specious reasoning. Technology does not automatically render obsolete the principles we found good in the past; they belong to a different order of things. Technology is not concerned with them. Principles have to do with the way we marshal our inner resources, discipline our actions, and respond to the promptings of our conscience; with the ordering of our personal lives and of our relations with fellow citizens, both in private and in public life. They apply to human beings.

Technology, on the other hand, deals with material things. Technology can enlarge our powers of mind and body. With it we can improve health, produce material abundance, leisure and comfort, circle the earth with instant communications, etc. But technology

does not dictate either the manner in which we put it to use, or

the specific benefits we want to derive from it.

I suggest we reject the notion that man is no longer master of his own and of his society's destiny. Let us put man back in the center of the stage and do some hard thinking about the kind of life technology is currently creating for us and how we might improve it, Let us not forget that under our political system it is the sovereign

citizen who, jointly with his fellow citizens, decides whether or

not to use his government for the purpose of minimizing harmful uses of technology and maximizing the benefits that can be derived from it.

We now possess scientific knowledge of such dangerous potential that its use can no longer be safely left to private decision . alone. In the past it was considered part of the American way of life to permit private owners to despoil our Continent. It will cost the American taxpayer a pretty penny to undo the damage; much of it can never be undone. Only now are we beginning to realize that careless use of dangerous pesticides and weed killers may poison soil, vegetation, animals and humans. It took the tragic case of the European thalidomide babies to dramatize the fatal consequences that may result if inadequately tested drugs are sold to an unsuspecting public.

The Founding Fathers were fully aware of the inestimable advantage our geographic isolation gave us: we had a chance to develop this country without fear of foreign invasion, without need of huge military establishments and of compulsory military service. We used to think their absence was a special virtue of the American way of life, Now we know they were a gift of Providence. De Tocqueville noted that "Fortune which has conferred so many peculiar benefits upon the inhabitants of the United States has placed them in the midst of a wilderness, where they have so-to-speak no neighbors." We now have the world's biggest military-industrial power complex. President Eisenhower warned us in his Farewell Address that so great a concentration of power is potentially a danger to democratic institutions. More serious in its effects upon individual freedon is another development not foreseen by the Founding Fathers. This is the rise of giant organizations which interpose themselves between the citizen and his government. When the nation was founded we did not even have political parties; there were only seven commercial corporations in all the colonies. We had no labor unions, vocational or professional associations, special interest groups or huge government bureaucracies. The citizen faced his government directly--whether local, state or federal. With his fellow citizens he shared control over his government on a basis of complete equality.

Today the citizen's wishes may be thwarted if they conflict with the interests of powerful organizations able to bring their immense resources to bear on government. Organizations have on the whole been successful in preventing governmental action they consider harmful to their interests, or at least in delaying action or rendering it innocuous. They also have been able to obtain special government benefits and privileges not vouchsaved ordinary citizens. Their ability to diminish the government's responsiveness to the wishes of the unorganized individuals who make up the electorate is indicative of great inequality of political power within the ‘constituency of our democratic government; this is a contingency the Founding Fathers could not have foreseen. the autonomy of the individual is diminished,

Many organizations are exclusively concerned with serving their members and do not seek to influence anyone; others are small and therefore a threat to no one; not a few are big and powerful but exemplary in behavior and performance. These do not concern us here. What does concern us is the threat to individual freedom posed by huge power complexes that dominate our lives but over which we are not able to exercise control. Among them are public as well as private organizations. Large government bureaucracies are as indispensable to modern society as are large private organizations. Many government tasks have to be entrusted to special agencies set up for this purpose. “Although they are a part of government, the citizen's influence over them is not as effective as it is on the elected branches of government; nor are the men who run these bureaucracies as accessible to citizens who have legitimate business with them as are elected government | officials. Organizations act through men. In our country, they act through a special category of career men, called managers or administrators, whose competence quite often lies exclusively in the field of “administration"; that is what they have been trained for. There is a curious analogy between the educational courses in administration, in leadership, in personnel management,

-etc., that make up the qualifications of the “pure" administrator, and the type of "training for rulership" that at one time used

to be given heirs to monarchical thrones. Mirabeau made the perceptive remark that "to administer...is to rule." ‘One might expand this to say that administration is a type of authoritarian rule with no constituency and no direct popular mandate. As

In nearly ali our large organizations administration stands apart and above production. The men who do the real work of the

organization are placed below the administrators who rule then.

Administrators May or may not have competence in the organization's

Special field of work; often they do not. In fact, this is considered so unimportant that “pure" administrators are freely moved from one type of organization to another. On the strength of their "ability to rule," administrators make all organization decisions, and, if they so desire, are free to meddle with the technical work of even the most highly qualified professionals in

the production end of the organization.

The concept of the "pure," technically untrained administrator as ruler of an organization is virtually unknown abroad. Take education: clearly the teachers do all the productive work but it is not they who manage the educational enterprise in this country. Teachers are bossed by administrators who quite often are not themselves teachers but whose training has been exclusively in management of the school's housekeeping, personnel and public relations chores. We are the only country that so debases the productive professional and SO exalts the

organization's "ruler."

The larger an organization, the more powerful are the managers or administrators. Their source of power comes from being in charge

of housekeeping matters, hence of the purse and so in turn of personnel

selection and promotion. Also they are at the communications center of the enterprise and know what is afoot. Since they represent the organization in external relations, they partake of its "image" and power.

Like all rulers, administrators are more important and better paid the larger their realm. Since they lack the distinction which productive men--whether technicians, professionals, scholars, or for that matter, artistic people--owe to their own personal competence, administrators identify with their organization. This is natural since it is only from control of the organization that they derive status. This makes them empire builders. It also accounts for a-tendency to authoritarianism.

Orders flow downward freely, but suggestions rarely rise upward in the hierarchy. Administrators also tend to be more interested in the organization as such than in the quality of its work. Outstanding achievements count for little if administrative routines have been violated. Administrators prefer personnel they can manipulate to mavericks whose initiative may produce some new process or attainment of enormous benefit to the organization.

The individual who must work in a large organization--private or public--meets conditions of inequality not found elsewhere in our democratic society. Nor is this inequality necessarily a result of unequal human qualities; it comes because one party has behind him the power of the organization and uses it to prevail over the other. This reminds one uncomfortably of the special rights and privileges which once were enjoyed by men for no other reason than membership in a higher estate-~

as when society was divided into the nobility, the clergy and the third estate.

Another point needs to be considered: The citizen can form no autonomous judgment on public issues unless he has access to relevant infomation permitting him to verify the facts and evaluate the merits of the case. Large organizations-- private and public--nowadays engage the services of highly trained public relations staffs whose job it is to project to the public as favorable an "image" of the organization as possible, and to hide its defects behind a screen of persuasive propaganda. Many people have been concerned over the danger of our becoming a pressure group state. It has been proposed that the people be given a special department to look after their interests. This misreads the problem. The government in all its branches is set up to look after the people's interests. To quote Madison, it "derives all its powers directly or indirectly from the great body of the people, and is administered by persons holding their offices during pleasure, for a limited period, or during good behaviour." |

"public policy." Let me explain.

Nearly all large private organisms owe their existence to corporate charters; they are creatures of the law, set up for specific purposes, and, to quote the Encyclopedia Brittanica, "restricted to actions concerned in furthering these purposes." Unlike natural persons who may do anything not forbidden by law, these synthetic persons may do only what the law expressly authorizes them to do. "Actions outside that field are ultra vires and can be restrained and declared void," The distinction is important for in actual fact it is nearly always ultra vires acts of organizations that adversely affect individual freedom. This is as true of so-called “membership corporations" as of "stock corporations."

With few exceptions, all successful democracies are today to be found in the West. All face Similar problems. All seek to solve them through the democratic process. Each can learn something from the others without being untrue to itself. There is, I believe, an irresistible trend toward changing government from the "night watchman" to the "service agency" type. Something could be learned from the way this transition is being made -in the most successful of European democracies. Their reasons have been practical, not ideological, and the process of transformation has been democratic.
