Summary

This document details the Theodore Roosevelt Centennial Commission's 1958 press releases, announcing the nationwide celebration of TR's 100th birthday, highlighted by President Eisenhower's call for renewed American citizenship and a special dinner honoring Admiral Hyman G. Rickover and General Alfred M. Gruenther with the Theodore Roosevelt Distinguished Service Medal. Rickover, famously called "Washington's most bristling and fruitful genius," and Gruenther, whose "soft speaking" was likened to TR's "compelling Big Stick," were recognized for their pivotal contributions to national defense, embodying Roosevelt's enduring ideals of public service, responsibility, and unwavering effort—qualities celebrated throughout the centennial year to reemphasize responsible citizenship. The releases also cite TR's famous quote, "The only life worth living is the life of effort...to attain what is worth striving for," and list past medal recipients including President Eisenhower, General Douglas MacArthur, and Herbert Hoover, underscoring the prestigious nature of the award and the broad impact of TR's legacy.

Of 26th fresident, teddy roosevelt

The nation will honor one of its most colorful Fresidents, Theodore Roosevelt, today, on the 100th anniversary of his birth.

Civic observances will be held throughout the country as Americans pause to pay tribute to their 26th President. The observances will mark the climax of a year-long centennial celebration which has been directed by a special Commission established by Congress.

Fresident Eisenhower issued a special Proclamation earlier in the year in which he called upon the American people to observe the 100th anniversary of TR's birth "by appropriate activities and ceremonies, by the study of his life and teachings, and above all, by individual, personal rededication to those responsibilities of American citizenship which he so zestfully fulfilled."

High point in the day's activities will be a dinner at Theodore Roosevelt's birthplace, 28 East 20th Street, New York, which is now headquarters for the Theodore Roosevelt Association.

General Alfred M. Gruenther and Vice Admiral Hyman G. Rickover will receive the Theodore Roosevelt Distinguished Service Medal, given by the Association.

The award was established in 1923 by the Association, "to perpetuate his memory and to promote the development and application of his ideals for the benefit of the American people." The medals are given each year to distinguished citizens who have rendered outstanding public service in one of thirteen fields. General Gruenther and Admiral Rickover are to be honored for their singular con- tributions to the National Defense.

General Gruenther, now president of the American Red Cross, capped a long and brilliant military career with his service as NATO Commander-in-Chief, suc- ceeding his long time friend, President Dwight D. Eisenhower. Admiral Rickover, still on active duty, is known as the "father of the atomic submarine,"

"It is a particular pleasure to have the privilege of honoring these two great Americans," said Oscar S, Straus, President of the Association, "for their devotion to the responsibilities of citizenship and to the nation's welfare are

typical of the qualities Mr. Roosevelt most admired,”

death of the 26th President, and was granted a national charter by Act of Congress In the course of its existence, the Association has purchased Theodore Roosevelt Island in the Potomac River in Washington, D. C., and presented it to the nation.
