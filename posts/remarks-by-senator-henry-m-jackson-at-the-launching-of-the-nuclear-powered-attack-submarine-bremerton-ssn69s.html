Summary

In his July 22, 1978 remarks at the launching of the nuclear-powered attack submarine USS Bremerton (SSN698) in Groton, Connecticut, Senator Henry M. Jackson emphasized the critical need for a strong U.S. Navy in the face of evolving global challenges. He lauded Admiral Hyman Rickover as the "guiding spirit" of the nuclear navy and noted a personal connection to the Bremerton, as its keel bore his wife Helen's initials. Jackson highlighted the alarming growth of Soviet naval power and its "expansionist mood" across Africa, Afghanistan, Yemen, and South Asia, contrasting this with the U.S. fleet's significant reduction to half its 1970 size and an inadequate shipbuilding program. Citing historical figures like Teddy Roosevelt's "speak softly and carry a big stick" advice and Lord Robert Cecil, the Senator underscored the Navy's vital role in safeguarding peace, ensuring freedom, maintaining sea lines of communication for Western economies, and supporting allies, concluding with a powerful quote from Henry Wadsworth Longfellow.

Saturday, July 22, 1978

REMARKS BY SENATOR HENRY M. JACKSON

At the Launching of the Nuclear Powered
Attack Submarine BREMERTON (SSN698)

Groton, Connecticut
Saturday, July 22, 1978
11:00 A.M.

Two years and two months have passed since the Jackson family
had the pleasure of joining in the keel-laying ceremonies for the
U.S.S. BREMERTON. During that time, many things have changed on
land and at sea. But one thing is unaltered: Hyman Rickover is
still the guiding spirit of our nuclear navy! I am delighted to
have my old friend Admiral Rickover here with us today as we welcome

his latest contribution to the fleet.

It is an honor to be in Groton once more to take part in the
christening of the new BREMERTON. As you know, the Jacksons feel a
special attachment to this ship. She bears not only a proud Navy

name, a proud name from the State of Washington, but also, upon her

q keel, the initials of my wife, Helen.
You recall Teddy Roosevelt's famous advice in 1903: "Speak
softly and carry a big stick." Did you know he was talking about

the need for a strong navy?

In that same speech he went on to advise the American Nation to

speak softly, "and yet build and keep at a pitch of the highest

training, a thoroughly efficient navy."

Teddy Roosevelt was on the right track way back then. Deeds,

not words, are what really count.

As Lord Robert Cecil put it: '"Vehemence of language falls
dead and impotent upon minds penetrated with the conviction that
the storm which is assailing them is nothing but words -- brave

words possibly, but still only words."

A strong, up-to-date Navy remains a fundamental requirement of

our national security.

Recent developments on the world scene emphasize this require-

ment:

* Soviet leaders are in an expansionist mood. They are on the
political offensive. In past probings, the strategic inferiority
of Soviet power and a restricted naval capacity has set definite
limits to the extent of the risks that Moscow was willing to run.
Today, with the strategic balance tilting in their favor and with
their growing naval forces, the Soviet leaders are accepting a more
extensive and dangerous range of risks in advancing their position.
The growing Soviet boldness is evident in a policy of military
adventure in Africa, coups in Afghanistan and Yemen, and provocative

moves in South Asia.

* The Western nations increasingly depend on foreign sources of

energy and raw materials. To a greater extent than ever before,

we rely on oil and other natural resources shipped across the oceans.
Disruption of these flows would have an immediate and devastating

effect upon Western economies.

* As the Soviet conventional threat has grown in Europe, the

credibility of the NATO deterrent increasingly depends on our ability
to keep the sea lines of communication open for massive, rapid

reinforcement of our allies in case of need.

* Long-standing American allies, including Japan, the Philippines
and South Korea, look to a strong U.S. naval presence in the Western
Pacific to support their independence. And we and the People's
Republic of China share parallel interests in deterring Soviet

expansionism in the Pacific area.

* Meanwhile, we see a continued growth in Soviet naval power.
The Soviet Union now possesses the world's largest Navy. A central
feature of its mission is the interdiction of Western sea lines

of communication.

A large, flexible, two-ocean American navy is essential in
the national interest. Yet, we are approaching the point where

our Navy is too small to perform vital missions.

Today our fleet is roughly one-half the size it was in 1970.
Through block obsolescence substantial numbers of World War II-
vintage ships have been only partially replaced by more capable,
more sophisticated, and much more costly vessels. Administration

after administration has reduced our five-year ship-building

program to the point where, this year, the President requested

for the construction of only fifteen ships. This building

program falls far short of what we need for the years ahead.

The Congress has moved to authorize a fourth Nimitz-class
carrier. This is the kind of determined signal the Soviets can
understand. The 13-carrier force of our Navy is the foundation for
our capability to support the NATO flanks, to maintain the freedom
of the seas and the supply lines to our friends and allies in
Europe, the Middle East and the Pacific, and to protect and assure
the sources and transportation of the free world's oil supplies into

the next century.

This ship we launch today is, inseparable from the skilled Naval
personnel that will operate it. We must rigorously maintain the
high standards of excellence in training and in operating that have

been the special distinction of the nuclear fleet.

The BREMERTON, and her sister nuclear ships, will sail the
seas as a reminder that a sovereign America is determined to

safeguard the peace and to assure the future of freedom.
As Henry Wadsworth Longfellow put it:

"Sail forth into the sea, O ship!

Through wind and wave, right onward steer?
Thou, too, sail on, O Ship of State?

Sail on, O UNION, strong and great!
Humanity with all its fears,

With all the hopes of future years,

Is hanging breathless on thy fate!"
