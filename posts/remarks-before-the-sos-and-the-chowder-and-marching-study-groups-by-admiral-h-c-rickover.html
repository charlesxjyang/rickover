Summary

In a July 15, 1970 address to the SOS and Chowder and Marching Study Groups, Admiral Rickover, identifying as an "amateur historian," offers insights into American political parties by drawing parallels between the 1970s and historical realignments, specifically Washington's first administration and the 1850s. He posits that a party's enduring success hinges on its ability to align with the core beliefs and frustrations of a stable "middle American" majority, rather than relying on coalitions of minorities. Rickover illustrates this through the failures of the Federalists and Whigs, contrasting them with the successes of Jefferson and Madison's Republicans and later Lincoln's Republican Party, which championed "free labor" and the "Protestant Ethic." He advises that, like the 1860 Republicans who appealed to self-reliant, hardworking citizens, today's party should represent the "middle segment" that values law and order, quality education, individual responsibility, and fair taxation, while advocating for reforms like public campaign financing. Rickover also references Carl Becker and Carl Schurz.

Remarks before the
SOS and the Chowder and Marching Study Groups
Washington, D. C.
July 15, 1970

il 5

I am no historian, but American history -- history in general--has fascinated
me all my life. Despite a heavy work schedule, I have managed to turn myself
into a fairly knowledgeable amateur. In any event, I find that current issues
are Clarified for me by looking at them in the light of history. I am struck with
the similarity of the present to the past, not with its differences as so many
others see it, especially the young who have learned very little history in our
school system and think the past is not "relevant." Observing history being
made all around me, and reading how it was made in the past, has led me to
certain convictions about the American party system. Whether they could be of
practical help, I cannot judge. But here they are.

I strongly believe that no party has an assured and long future unless it
establishes:‘a convincing, clearly stated connection with the troubles and
frustrations, as well as the aims and aspirations, and basic beliefs of a
relatively stable majority of the American people. To sense the formation of
a new majority is the secret of the successful party. To sense the erosion of
an existing majority and profit thereby is part of that secret. I would judge we

are currently in a situation somewhat resembling both the period at the end

of Washington's first administration and during the 1850's.

The Federalists who were chiefly responsible for the creation of our
Federal Union and who organized the central government under Washington
and Hamilton, failed to convince a majority of the people that their policies--
by enhancing the strength of the Nation politically and economically--were in
fact directly beneficial to them as individuals.

The overwhelming majority of Americans at that time were self-sufficient
farmers producing little beyond the needs of their families and perhaps a local
market, or they had small shops or were artisans or fishermen. It was not
immediately evident to them how they could personally profit from Federalist
efforts to build a strong national economy, to secure the credit of the Nation,
and to provide a stable currency. It was therefore extremely easy for
Jefferson and Madison to win a majority for their new anti-Federalist party--
then called the Republican Party, as you all know--by accusing the opponents
of "monarchical" or "aristocratic" tendencies, of being concerned only with the
rich and not with the ordinary citizen. On their famous "botanizing" expedition
to New York in 1791, Jefferson and Madison forged a coalition of southern
planters, western farmers and the poorer segments of northern city people which
proved extraordinarily durable. Defeating the Federalists in 1800, the
Jeffersonian Republicans controlled the Federal Government for almost three
decades, ruling with virtually no national opposition.

The party held together until 1828, when its larger part formed a new coalition
of farmers and mechanics under Jackson who called it the "Democracy." Clay

rallied the dissident remnants in a new party, the Whigs, whose appeal was more

broadly based than that of the Federalists but whose policies were essentially

Hamilton's brought up-to-date. The Whigs wanted to build a large domestic
market which would make the country economically independent--tariffs to
promote domestic industry, federally aided internal improvements to facilitate
overland transport, sound money and a national bank. They, too, failed to
convince a stable majority that these were policies benefiting them. Jackson
had no difficulty making a case against the Whigs on the grounds of allegedly
favoring the rich, the “eastern money" power. Winning occasional victories,
the Whigs never held power long enough to carry out their economic program.
The seesaw between them and the Democrats down to the Civil War was not
unlike the situation we have had since the early 1930's.

Yet the very policies espoused first by the Federalists, then by the Whigs,
formed a major part of the platform on which the newly formed Republican Party
rode to victory with Lincoln in 1860, and maintained itself in power for half a
century thereafter.

I am no expert in political matters; indeed I have no business talking to
experts like yourselves on these matters. But if I were concerned with trying
to build up your party nationally so that we should have a close equilibrium, with
power shifting frequently between the two great parties--more frequently and more
completely than in the past 40 years--if this were my job, I should study very
carefully how the Republicans of 1860 managed to convince the majority that
theirs was a "popular," not an "elite" or "aristocratic" or "rich man's" party.
I suspect it was because they sensed that a large majority in the North were

tired of government by a coalition of minorities. I think it may well be that

something like this is taking place today. Superficially, everything seems

different--today's Americans are different people, the divisive issues are
not sectional. But I would guess, that, as of 1860, a majority is in process
of formation which does not feel itself fully represented by either of today's
two parties.

It is generally thought that our political system functions best when both
parties share a large body of similar beliefs and objectives. As Carl Becker
said, government by majority performs most successfully when political issues
do not tou ~ - deep social and economic divisions. The reason of course is
that the minority then finds it relatively easy to accept defeat at the polls--

a lost election does not mean "a fatal surrender of vital interests." This is
true enough in normal times, but not when there is a deep rift in the body
politic. Such a rift cannot be bridged unless the parties accurately reflect the
opposing views so that conflicts may be settled by majority decision instead

of being allowed to linger on, imperfectly resolved by negotiated compromises--
as was the case during the three decades preceding the Civil War.

Slavery--with all its political, economic and social ramifications--was, of
course, the cause of the rift, of the growing alienation between North and South.
Yet neither of the two parties took a clear-cut stand on this issue; both evaded
it in order to remain "national" parties. Third parties were formed, but they
were insignificant and short-lived, none offering a program broad enough to
appeal to the majority of "middle" Americans. None, that is, until the advent

of the Republicans.

Combining as it did the economic policies of the Whigs with a promise of
homestead legislation (steadfastly voted down by the southern-controlled
Democrats) and with a straightforward ban on further extension of slavery into
the territories (an old Federalist objective), the 1860 platform of the Republican
Party represented the first commitment in years to the interests of a broad
segment of the population of the North where there were as yet few social or
economic differences and most enterprises were still owner operated. It
therefore appealed to independent mill owners, merchants and farmers, as much
as to those who w ~ ° for others but had ambitions to become independent--for
them the realization of these ambitions was tied to keeping slavery out of the
federal territories and making the land available to "free labor" as a safety
valve, should industrial conditions worsen, and as an ever present opportunity
for everyone willing to face the risk of pioneer life in order to achieve economic
independence.

Of those who abandoned old party affiliations and voted Republican in 1860--
and for many decades thereafter--few were rich. Indeed, as is true of most
"middle" Americans then and now, great wealth and the power it wields were
anathema to them. They disliked rich northern mill owners--the so-called
"lords of the loom"--as much as southern slave holding planters--the "lords
of the lash." But what I believe they disliked more than anything else was the
coalition of these two which to them was personified in the Democratic Party--
then no longer Jackson's party of the common man--no less then in the Whigs

who put up no strong opposition, being rendered ineffective by their desire to

hold on to their southern constituents. In contrast, the Republicans never

ceased denouncing the "slave power" and its control of the Executive, the
Senate, and the Supreme Court, especially the use by the southern minority
of the 5th Amendment--as in the Dred Scott case--to bolster their demand for
the extension and national protection of slavery. The party never tired of
glorifying the dignity of "free labor," setting it against its debasement where-
ever slavery existed. Carl Schurz, endorsing Lincoln's nomination in 1860,
summed it all up in the words: "The Republicans stand before the country not
only as the antislavery party, but emphatically as the party of free labor."

The term "free labor then applied to all who worked with their own hands
and brains to support themselves and their families. America was to them the
country of opportunity for the common man, the one country in the world where
he controlled his government. They were, if you will, a "silent majority"
become vociferous when they felt that a coalition of minorities above and below
"middle" America was perverting the Union of the Pounding Fathers. They
believed the society of the North exemplified the best in America, that of the
South the worst--a conviction confirmed in every major Republican campaign
speech. To them, the determination of the new party to capture the Federal
Government and thus end the minority rule of the "slave power" meant a return
to the cardinal principles of the American political system, the rule of the
majority.

It was this clear-cut commitment to a middle class society, composed of
self-reliant people coming up from the ranks of labor, none very rich, but

hardworking, with faith in education and what has come to be called the

"Protestant Ethic"--it was this that distinguished the Republicans of 1860 from
the Federalists and Whigs with whom they shared their whole economic philo-
sophy. Only when those favoring a strong nation, both economically and
politically, tied this Philosophy to the aims of "middle" Americans did they win

a stable and continuing majority. Noone since Lincoln--except perhaps

Theodore Roosevelt--succeeded SO well in convincing "middle" America that

One might dismiss this as ancient history, but I feel certain that without
emulating this identification of Purpose no dependable majority support can be
built. The pa =r most natural to the Republican Party is identification with
"middle" America. It Cannot be avowedly "conservative" for, in the context
of American political values, this would brand it as a party of the rich, Nor,
in my judgment, would it have a secure future if it left itself open to the
accuSation of disregarding the interests of "middle" Americans in favor of one
Or another or a combination of minorities. This would be to build on Sand--at
least in the long run, [ may be wrong, but I believe that a large and Stable
middle segment is evolving in our Society--not altogether unlike the constituency
of the Party in 1860 and for decades thereafter, the segment your Party
is inherently best equipped to represent.

Ranging through every economic level, it consists of People who are
competent in their own sphere of activity, hardworking, able to make their way
on their own. They pay most of the taxes yet receive Proportionately the least
benefit from them since, asa group, they are given few if any special Privileges--

no federal Subsidies for them for non-work or plain incompetence, no tax

write offs, no Government contracts yielding high profits--no handouts from

the Government. The issues which today loom large in the eyes of these

people involve no demands on their part for special benefits; they are issues
concerning all Americans who contribute to society and are fundamentally in
accord with our political system. Such as the need to devise new ways to
finance political campaigns out of public funds so that candidates need be
neither rich nor obligated to large campaign contributors. To bring about this
reform today would be as great a party achievement as was the ban on slavery in
the territories in 1860--then as now, effecting a return to the fundamental concept
of our political system; government chosen by and accountable to the people or,
more precisely, to the current majority of the people. Or take the long over-
due reform of our school system. What "middle" Americans want is good

education, not schooling that is used primarily for social experimentation..

They are for law and order in the sense that every civilized person wants it--even-
handed administration of justice with no privileged status for anyone; competent
and honest police; laws made by the representatives of the elected majority not
by self-righteous minorities. They are against the parasites of society whether
they be the very rich who escape taxation by gimmickry or the very poor who
demand, as they see it, too large a slice of their own hard earned income in
taxes.

I need not pursue this further, except to remark, in passing, that it is these
people who have contributed an outsize share of men to defend the nation in war.
To see the flag trampled and the United States derided, destroys the spiritual

basis of their lives, makes a mockery of their beliefs. I suspect that this
