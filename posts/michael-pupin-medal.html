Summary

In a speech delivered upon receiving the Michael Pupin Medal at Columbia University in October 1958, Admiral H.G. Rickover passionately champions Western education as the singular force behind human progress and the dynamism of Western civilization. He presents Michael Pupin's life as an exemplary immigrant success story, nurtured by a liberal education spanning the US, Cambridge, and Berlin, to underscore the "common heritage" shared between America and Europe. Rickover asserts that the West's higher living standards and scientific miracles, such as penicillin, stem from European inventions like formal education, public libraries, universities, and academic freedom. He sharply contrasts this with pre-revolutionary China's examination system, arguing it fostered "mental ossification" and stagnation, leading to a society incapable of adapting to population growth and ultimately resulting in the brutal pragmatism exemplified by Mao Zedong. Rickover refutes the idea that Western civilization is merely materialistic, highlighting its contributions to human well-being. He concludes by criticizing contemporary American educational trends, which he labels "life-adjustment, behavioral conditioning, and a leveling downwards," urging a return to a robust liberal arts curriculum (echoing "Delenda est life-adjustment conditioning" from Cato the Elder) that fosters independent thought and is aligned with the comprehensive educational ideals articulated by Theodore Roosevelt.

A hundred years ago this, month, Francis Joseph, Emperor of
Austria and Hungary, acquired still another rebellious Serbian sub-
ject. Fifteen years later, this subject had enough of the stifling
air of stagnation which pervades dying societies. A chance acquaint-
ance with two traveling Americans had convinced the young man that in
the New World he would find an invigorating atmosphere of freedom.

So he sold his school books, his sheepskin coat and cap, and sailed
across the seas. Up to a point, Pupin's was the typical "poor |
immigrant makes good in America" success story. He arrived here, a

red fez on his head, with five cents in his pocket, and immediately

went to work at whatever job he could find. Millions of others began

life in America in the same way.

But Pupin was a little unusual in that he had come here to
continue his education. From the first day he set himself to master
English and prepare for college. Atter, five years of hard physical
work and night-time study, he passed the entrance examinations with
honors and received a scholarship to Columbia College. At twenty-
five years of age, only three years older than the average American
student, he received his pachelor's degree from this institution.

No mean achievement this, when you consider the difficulties he had
to overcome: Lack of funds, the need to master a new language,
adjustment to mores as different from those of rural Banat on the
Austrian frontier as one could imagine!

By tutoring other students and with the help of scholarships,
Pupin was able to continue graduate study in mathematics and physics.
He was advised that the best place for such study were the famous
universities of Europe. He therefore returned to Europe and studied
first at Cambridge, then at Berlin where he received his doctoral
degree. Upon his return to America, he joined the faculty of
Columbia University where he remained for the rest of his active
academic life. He beseme one of America's great physicists and in-
ventors. His primary field of interest was electrical communications.
One of his great inventions brought about a reduction of attenuation
and distortion in long-distance telephone communications and commem-
orizes his name. In Europe, telepone lines using his invention are

called "pupinized" lines.

In the making of this great inventor we can observe a happy

synthesis of the most diverse educational influences. Begun in a
Serbian village school, continued in secondary schools in the Voyvodins
and in Bohemian Prague, and thereafter in an American college, com-
pleted at the universities of Cambridge in England and Berlin in
Germany - Pupin's education symbolizes the basic unity of academic
life in all countries of Western civilization. This unity is a precious
thing which cannot, to my mind, be overemphasized. The centennial of
his birth offers an opportunity to reaffirm: the common heritage we
share with Europe, whence have come the ancestors of nearly all living
Americans, The spiritual and intellectual ties which bind the New
World to the Old have been woven by millions of immigrants - not many
of them Pupins, it is true, but all carrying with them across the
seas something, however minuscule, which has gone into the making of
America. Without our common intellectual heritage, and the unity of
our basic outlook on life, there would be no substance in the joining
together of our country with Europe by the North Atlantic Pact. There
would be little assurance that this alliance will be firm enough to
resist aggression through the iron curtain which divides two diametri-
cally opposed civilizations. In a pamphlet issued in 1949, our State
Department says of the Pact that it "has its roots in the common
heritage and civilization of the peoples living on both shores of the
North Atlantic Ocean."
