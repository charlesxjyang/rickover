Summary

This document transcribes a March 26, 1969, House Armed Services Committee hearing focused on a critical retention crisis for nuclear-qualified submarine officers, a problem Admiral Hyman G. Rickover had long highlighted. The central issue is the severe shortage of these highly trained personnel, with figures indicating a 50% resignation rate in certain year groups, 370 total resignations, and an active manning requirement of over 1,400 officers against a total community of less than 2,000, compounded by an inadequate training pipeline (only 600 in training versus 950 needed). To address this, the committee considers H.R. 9328, a bill proposing special pay of up to $3,750 annually for officers agreeing to a four-year service extension, totaling up to $15,000, emphasizing the urgent need to retain these vital experts to ensure the continued manning of Polaris and nuclear attack submarines.

The Navy, as of early February 1969, had in hand a ‘total of 370

_esignations from regular, nuclear trained submarine officers, plus 44

scheduled releases from Active Duty Reserve officers. Resignation rates from these nuclear trained officers in year groups 1961 through 1964 approach, 50 percent, with new resignation requests being received at a rate of approximately 30 per month.

The Navy tells me that 300 nuclear submarine officers are scheduled for release from active duty in calendar year 1960, and if present resignation deferral policy (1-year delay) were relaxed, at least 100 more would be released.

This alarming resignation rate becomes more meaningful when we realize that most of these resignations are coming from relatively junior officers who represent the only possible source for future depart- ment heads and commanding officers for our nuclear attack and Polaris submarines.

These are the circumstances which have resulted in the legislative proposal which we have before us today. It is not the only action being

taken by the Navy to cope with this problem, but it represents, in the -

judgment of the executive branch, the most expeditious and meaningful short-term solution to the retention problem.

In addition to requesting approval of authority to provide a con- tinuation pay bonus to nuclear-trained submarine officers, the Chief of Naval Operations, after meeting with his subordinate commanders and the Chief of Naval Personnel, has initiated a number of other

administrative actions which hopefully will somewhat alleviate the problem.

Among the steps taken are an effort to reduce substantially the ad- ministrative burdens and paperwork heretofore confronting officers in the nuclear program, as well as an intensified recruiting effort to secure new qualified applicants for the program.

Our witnesses today can, of course, elaborate on these efforts made by the Navy.

The bill before us would provide the Secretary of the Navy with authority to pay a continuation pay bonus to nuclear qualified sub- marine officers who have not completed 10 years of commissioned service, and who agree to voluntarily extend their period of active service.

The bonus will ordinarily be payable to these nuclear submarine officers upon completion of their first sea tour—in return for a 4-year active service commitment. The bonus, however, will be payable to every nuclear-qualified submarine officer with less than 10 years of commissioned service providing he had agreed to remain in the active submarine service for one period of 4 years beyond his obligated perod of service. The bonus can in no case exceed $3,750 for each year of the active service agreement, with a maximum therefore of $15,000 for the 4-year period.
