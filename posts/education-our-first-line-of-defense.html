Education - Our First Line of Defense

1958 Speech Source: USNA Archives Box 30
View Original PDF View Original TXT

Summary

In this December 1958 speech, VADM H. G. Rickover vehemently argues that education is "our first line of defense," a realization sharpened by the shock of Sputnik, which exposed America's educational deficiencies. He critiques the prevailing "life-adjustment" philosophy in U.S. schools, which he claims undervalues intellectual competence and resists external criticism from non-professional "educationists," citing figures like Robert M. Hutchins, Aristotle, and DeTocqueville on the importance of public discourse and education. Rickover highlights the "inane trivialities" of some education theses (e.g., "School Camping in New Jersey," "Baseball Ability") and contrasts American standards with the higher academic rigor and longer schooling hours of European nations and the USSR, which he attributes to their superior intellectual achievements and commitment to education as national advancement. He concludes by urging American colleges to raise admission standards to compel public schools to improve, echoing Whitehead's warning that "the race which does not value trained intelligence is doomed."

EDUCATION - OUR FIRST LINE OF DEFENSE

Since you invited me to discuss my views on American
education, I take it for granted that you share my anxiety over
its present state and that you believe with me that what goes on
in the nation's classrooms is a matter of legitimate concern to
every American which he has a right to discuss in public. Robert
M. Hutchins warns that "an uncriticized culture will not endure,"
and Aristotle, writing in the twilight of Athenian democracy,
reminds us that education is every nation's most important business.
The education of youth, says this most famous of Plato's pupils,
is not merely one among the many concerns to which the lawmaker
must "direct his attention," but the primary one, "for the neglect
of education ruins the constitution of the state,"

Hutchins and Aristotle are separated in time by more than two
thousand years but their thinking is close because they are both
firmly rooted in Western civilization. Their two Statements sum

up the theme of my speech,

It took the shock of Russia's Sputniks to bring us to full
realization that today the rate of a country's technological
progress depends on the quality of its educational system. Not
until this shattering of our complacency did we face up to the
reality of America's educational deficiencies and talk freely
about them in public. We are only beginning to fully grasp the
fact that education is our first line of defense which we neglect
at our peril.

We might well ask ourselves why an external stimulus was
needed to scare us into giving serious thought to the education
of our children. Why had we previously paid no attention to ‘
those--and they were not a few--who deplored the deterioration
of scholastic standards in our schools and warned that Russia's
massive upgrading of her education presented a real danger to.
our position of world leadership?

I submit that we were caught by surprise because we did
not accord education the place of importance it deserves in our
private lives and in public affairs, and because we did not
keep ourselves informed on current educational theories and
practices through public debate of this all-important national
issue.

-Of course, in times of widespread material well-being,
people tend to be apathetic about matters which do not seem to

affect their personal comfort and security. This is natural.

When things run smoothly, nobody goes around looking for trouble.

I believe, however, that we in this country are particularly

loath to make national problems our personal business and that
we are abetted in this--if not actually pressured into it--by
diverse special interest groups which would like us to leave to
the "experts" all matters that specially interest them.

We are easily intimidated by pressure groups. some
important issues are not freely discussed in public for fear
of displeasing such groups. This is not a new thing in this
country. As long ago as 1831, DeTocqueville remarked with
astonishment that "freedom of opinion does not exist in America,"
and Theodore Roosevelt--the greatest teacher of democracy this
country ever had--spoke in forceful language against minority
groups and warned that we could not tolerate being torn apart
by selfish group interests. Despite Roosevelt's warning, I doubt
we have fewer minority groups today than existed in his time.
Some have disappeared, but new ones have taken their places;
many have shown great hardihood and seem to live on forever.
Common to all these groups is dislike of public discussion of
their particular interests; they realize they cannot win public
approval by persuasion. Some groups seek to obtain selfish
advantages which run counter to national interest. Others
sincerely believe that they know best what is good for the people.
Among the latter are to be found groups of professional experts
whose work is of a specialized nature or who think that it is.

Knowledge increases so rapidly today that extreme specialization

4s inevitable. This could easily raise a parrier between the

mass of the people on one side and numerous small groups of
experts on the other; each little group of specialists seeking

undemocratic viewpoints to prevail. One does not have to be an

The American people are now thoroughly aroused about our
schools and are forcefully expressing their concern, put powerful
educationists are determined to maintain the Chinese wall which
they have built around American education. Educational isolation
of this country has no counterpart in other advanced nations of
the West. Both official and non-official surveys of the
educational systems of other countries are published regularly
in Evropean countries. We have a federal agency which is charged
with disseminating educational data, but I am not aware of
similar surveys being put out by them. The only exception seems
to be the "Report on Education in the U.S.S.R." which was
published by the Department of Health, Education and Welfare in
mid-1957. At that, the report was unaccountably delayed,
according to its author. Disquieting evidence of Russia's
educational progress had been accumulating for eight years prior

to its publication.

Wholly erroneous views of the educational standards and

practices of other countries are unfortunately widespread among
American educationists. Lack of interest in education abroad--
which is dismissed as being aristocratic and hence unsuitable
to this country--prevents our schools from benefiting by the
educational experiences of others. What is done in schools
elsewhere may be well worth our attention since it can often be
adapted to specific American needs. Educationist parochialism
becomes a positive danger to the country when it prevents the
leaders of American education from keeping abreast of developments
in Russia. Indeed they have been slow to perceive the challenge
of Russian education and even slower to admit the threat it
represents to our country. In no other way can one explain the
amazement and shock publicly expressed by prominent professional
educators who finally went to Russia this year to check for
themselves. |

As far back as 1954, people outside education had warned of
the tremendous educational development in Russia and had urged
that we put our own educational system in order. These warnings
were airily dismissed as "hysterical" and the warners were told
not to interfere in affairs about which they could know nothing--
not having taken the prescribed credit courses in "education,"
one must presume! I suppose we should be thankful that at last
there has now been a change of viewpoint among some of these
recent travelers. "We were simply not prepared for the degree to

which the. U.S.S.R., as a nation, is committed to education as a

5

means of national advancement," said one of them. But not every

was present the type of truculent attitude which is represented
by statements such as: I'm here to collect evidence that will
prove American schools are superior to the Russian. This reflects
the all too common educationist view that the schools are their
private business which no one can be permitted to criticize. We
have the right to expect of educators that they seek the truth
and have the courage to face it. Once the truth is known, there
is nothing to keep this country from surpassing any foreign
educational system.

The attempt of leading educationists to keep public education
a preserve of the "professional" educator lowers the tone of the
great educational debate which has been going on since Sputnik.
I believe one may fairly say that most critics of American
education argue on a level of ideas and facts. They point out
where the public, the parents, and the educators have gone wrong,
for obviously there would be no educational crisis if there had
been no human failures. But rarely do the critics make the
educator the sole scapegoat. They do, however, point out what
specific educational theories and practices must be changed if
we are to bring excellence to American public education; and
where educational reform has been blocked by educationists, they
pring out this fact. How else are we to make a change for the

petter? .

On the other hand, all too many educationists stoop to the
level of personal invective in order to discredit the critics
of American education instead of answering the specific
criticisms. I have yet to see a well-reasoned, well-documented
counterargument which takes up every point raised by the eritics
and attempts to prove them wrong. Instead, the whole issue
becomes a duel between educationist and eritic in which the
educationist is concerned solely with his amour-propre and his
job, and the critic is accused of being an ignoramus out to
make a lot of money by irresponsible sensationalism. The rights
of our children are lost in this undignified attempt to silence
criticism at all cost.

What horrifies me is the apparent inability of many
educationists to conceive the possibility that one may criticize
American education for no other reason than because he is
sincerely concerned with the future of our youth; that it is
possible to criticize because one believes in American public
education, not because one hates it, as educationists imply3
that one may feel there are better educational philosophies than
John Dewey's without necessarily being a crackpot or a
reactionary. For me, our educational crisis is a matter of such
gravity that the heavy-handed vulgarity, the clumsy jocularity
to be found in some educationist repartee trouble me greatly.
The future of our children is not a fit subject for dialect

stories. What manner of men are these who degrade a serious

To an educationist, a person who is not a "professional"

documented, most clearly reasoned and most interestingly

them the inadequacy of the public schools is daily made manifest
by the educational deficiencies of students who must be given
remedial courses in high school--sometimes even elementary school
--subjects before they can be taught at the college level.

As one works his way through mountains of educationist

school men to be independent of the colleges. In this, they
differ greatly from secondary school educators abroad. There,
the universities set the standard for all education. The
academic secondary schools must prepare students to pass the
examination for the "leaving-certificate," without which students
are not admitted to universities; in turn, the high scholastic
standards of the academic secondary schools influence those of

standards are higher all around than in this country. We should

soon see marked progress if American colleges performed for

Parenthetically, it should be said that there is some

A favorite gambit used by educationists to discredit lay
eriticism deserves mention. The critic is asked: "Would you

This argument has superficial persuasiveness and seems to have

Most critics are pretty tough skinned because they are

motivated by a deep concern for our children's future. Even so,
making criticism unpleasant, even risky, can sometimes silence
it.

In his essay On Liberty, John Stuart Mill tells of an
interesting way in which this was accomplished by an ancient
people--the Locrians. Being as fond as the next man of
maintaining the even tenor of familiar customs and ways of life,
put desirous of giving lip service to the noble concept of free
speech, the Locrians devised a splendid method which allowed
them to eat their free speech cake and have it too. At public
meetings anyone could argue for a change in the laws and customs
but not before a halter had been placed around his neck. The
instant anything he said met with public disapproval, the rope
was pulled tight. Thus were the frivolous faultseeker and the
reforming crank kept in hand. There are times when I personally
have felt a sense of kinship with the Locrian critic!

Since it has been effective in discouraging public debate,
let us examine the educationists' "doctor gambit.” The analogy
between the "professional" expertise of our public school men
and that of our physicians is so tenuous as to render the gambit
pointless. One of our great educational deficiencies is the
meager professional qualification which suffices in this country
to admit anyone to employment in public education below the
college level. It is, in particular, quite inferior to that

required of secondary school teachers in other Western nations

who are almost as thoroughly trained as are members of other
"learned" professions. In this country, the "professional"
knowledge possessed by secondary school officials and teachers
cannot be compared with the "professional" knowledge possessed
by a licensed physician.

There is indeed a wide gap in medical knowledge between
physician and layman. But the required courses in "education"
which make up the American educatr's professional study program
are not such as to create a wide gap between him and the layman;
not, at any rate, if the layman has taken the trouble to inform
himself on educational theories, practices and objectives; not,
if the layman by reason of being an employer has had practical
experience with the products turned out by our schools! The
subject at issue is not so difficult to understand as we are led
to believe. Nor do we need to be overawed by academic degrees
in education for, alas, many are granted for intellectual
achievements which are not impressive.

particular stopped Flexner in his tracks. "I pause for a moment,"

Lest I be accused of talking about what happened in the past,

New Jersey," "The Predication of Baseball Ability Through an
Analysis of Selected Measures of Structure and Strength," "A

Colleges, and Universities in the United States."

Let us not be intimidated by the "doctor gambit." Public
education is a subject well within the grasp of the layman,
especially the educated layman. Moreover, it must be remembered
that in this country--as in most advanced Western nations--
education is for the most part a tax-supported enterprise and
everyone in it is a public servant. One of the important
differences between citizens of a democracy and subjects of
authoritarian states is that the democratic citizen has the

right as well as the duty to check and evaluate the performance

14

of public officials, and to express his dissatisfaction when

they do not carry out the mandate of the people or when they
discharge their functions unsatisfactorily. Sovereignty lodges
in the people, and public servants--whether elected or appointed;
federal, State, or local; career or civil service--are all agents
of the people. They will be Judged irrespective of whether or
not the critic possesses vocational competence equal to that of
the public servant. We criticize the police when it is derelict
in its duties though we may have no erl minological competence,

We need not be tax experts to publicly--ana loudly--express our
views on taxation. My own work is of a highly technical
character, but as a sovernment servant my work is at all times
Subject to public Scrutiny. It would be calamitous if things
ever came to pass in this country where every self-styled
"professional" expert could silence public discussion of his work,
Simply by claiming that no layman can understand what he is doing.
That way lies censorship which in the past, also, arose from
claims to infallibility, but which in civilized countries has had
to make way for the right of free men to Speak their minds,

The importance of free speech in a democracy cannot be
overstated, It is a privilege which may be lost if it is not
used. But more important, it is a necessity for insuring good
sovernment. In democracies public servants are not born to high
office, nor do they hold it by dint of possessing absolute power;
they are elected. They will be good or bad, depending on whether

the people who elect them are well-informed, independent in

their judgment, and patriotic enough to put the national interest

Public debates on national issues are an important part
of the democratic process. Through them the citizen is enabled
to familiarize himself with facts which he might not have had
the time or ability to dig up for himself. By hearing opposing
"experts" give their interpretations of these facts and their
solutions to the problem under discussion, the citizen discovers
all aspects of the issue and is helped to make wise decisions.

In any public debate, the more varied the viewpoints that are
aired, the better, Let us disregard the "professional"
qualificationsof those who wish to talk about education and
concentrate on what they say rather than on who they are.

Though I have never taken a course in "education," I have
been running schools for years, Everyone in my atomic power
group has taken a hand at some time or other at teaching what
he has first had to teach himself about the new science of
nuclear engineering. We have set up schools for our own
engineers and for industry people, as well as for the officers |
and men who will operate our nuclear ships. In our post-graduate
program we teach the exacting requirements of reactor design,

construction, and management. We, too, have come across

deplorable lacunae in the education of our students; like go many

Only a strong motive could have induced me to give up the little

As any engineer would, I noticed many years ago that Russia,

years during World War II to about ten years today--while in Russia

and rapidly as we must to keep ahead of Russia, unless we find ways

Nearly all these roadblocks are caused by people whose
education has not prepared them for the extremely difficult

faith in the miracles of science which permeates our national
mores and causes us to be overly optimistic. I believe it is
necessary that we become somewhat more realistic; that we revise

a gift dispensed by a scientific fairy godmother but something
which must be earned by upgrading of the total human personality.
Whenever we make an advance in man's age-old effort to utilize
the forces of nature, we must simultaneously raise our education,
our techniques, and our institutions to a higher plateau. The
civilized man has always had to meet higher demands on his total
humanity than the primitive man living in an archaic society.
Let me illustrate this by an example of past upgradings ina

If you obtain your power from human muscle, wind, or water,
you need almost no technical knowledge. To drive a gang of
galley slaves, to manage a windmill or water mill does not even

Schools that once prepared young people adequately for life

My personal experience in developing nuclear power has
convinced me that our schools do not produce enough qualified
people to run our complex technological Society. We have a
chronic shortage of trained professionals which is not limited

Because of these Shortages, our most competent people often are

less qualified man must often substitute. The result is either

unsatisfactory performance or costly delay. Our educational

scientific age. Much more education is needed today, not just

I began work on nuclear propulsion by setting out to find
men of independent mind and venturesome spirit to make up my
reactor group. From the start it was evident that no experienced
men of this type were available. Before we could seriously tackle

could not meet the requirements of the nuclear power project--
and hence inferentially of any new development project--vastly
outnumbered those who qualified.

This experience made a deep impression on me. It led me

directly to a study of why our educational system produces so

22

few men who are qualified to do the work which we must do if
we are to progress. I found that our schools are the greatest
"cultural lag" we have today.

When I read official publications put out by the men who
run our educational system--booklets such as " ife-Adjustment
Education for Every Youth" or "Education for All Youth" --I have
a strange feeling of reading about another world, a world long
since departed if it ever existed at all. I sense the kindly
spirit, the desire to make every child happy, the earnest

determination to give advice on every problem any young person

might ever meet in life--and withal so complete a misunderstanding

of the real needs of young people in today's world that it
frightens me. |

If I speak out against a mistaken concept of what twentieth
century American education should be, I do so out of no desire
to find fault with those who misread the demands of the times
but from anxiety for the future of our children. I am worried
about the chances which young people--who are so poorly equipped
to deal with modern life--will have when things become more
complex and difficult, as they surely will before very long.
Today's big problems for young people are not how to choose the
proper necktie, or how to be socially popular--these are minor
problems which any mother can easily teach her children; which
children can in fact pick up themselves as they associate with

others.

23

It is quite Ineomprehensible to me why so much emphasis is
put on teaching children to "adjust" to their "peer" group. A
child's best learning time is short and should be put to better
use. Let us help him to absorb real knowledge and not waste
his time teaching him something that comes naturally to all
children. Every parent knows that immature youngsters almost
slavishly conform to the mores of their contemporaries. They

certainly need no courses in how to become little "organization

men." The danger is rather that they may grow up without
learning to think for themselves. Overstressing group adjustment
may lead children to pick the wrong group and join a juvenile
gang. Members of such gangs are probably the most perfectly
group-adjusted youngsters you can find anywhere}

If education is to have any value it must help the youngster
to reach maturity; to develop his reasoning power; and, through
the discipline of rigorous study, to strengthen his character.
No one can predict what problems he will meet in this uncertain
and changeable life. The only way to prepare him so he can deal
with whatever challenge life may bring is to make sure that when
he graduates high school, he will be a truly "educated" young
person. By that I do not mean the same thing as some of our
educationists. Witness the statement of.a teachers college dean, :
quoted in John Keat's revealing book "Schools Without Scholars:"

"An educated man is one who is well adjusted and helpful in his

community." When the dean was asked "whether a man who was well

24

adjusted and helpful could be considered educated without also
being able to count his fingers or write his name," the dean
said, "Yes." __

Our schools have done a fine job making Americans out of
youngsters with diverse backgrounds and doing this in record
time. But this job is finished. The schools must now tackle a
different job. They must concentrate on bringing the
intellectual powers of each child to the highest possible level.
Even the average child now needs almost as good an education as
the average middle and upper-class child used to get in college
preparatory schools. The talented child needs special schooling
to move him rapidly through the period of absorbing knowledge
into the period when his fine mind can turn this knowledge into
new ideas, new discoveries, new ways of life. We need creative
thinkers in the humanities no less than in the sciences, Living
in crowded areas demands more of us in tolerance, in consideration,
and in acceptance of necessary rules than life on the frontier.
Perhaps our grandchildren will have to live with fewer material
possessions. It is not too early to turn to inner resources,
which are limitless: to art, music, literature, good
conversation; to cultivation of a more contemplative way of life.

Other countries have successfully carried through the most
drastic of educational reforms. So can we once we recognize
the need. There is much we can learn from the advanced nations

of Europe which have always stood in the forefront of education.

25

By any pragmatic test, their education has proved its value.
Crowded, resources-poor Europe still wields influence out of

all proportion to its relative size; it still has the highest
living standard, excepting only that of North America. These
are results of her remarkable intellectual achievements. I
invite you to read the roster of Nobel prize winners in physics
and chemistry; the number of original thinkers produced in the
last hundred years; the number of basic inventions for present-
| day technology; the discoveries in medicine which have made life
happier and safer--in all these you will find Europeans
outnumbering Americans several times over, measured in per onpiita
performance,

At the other end of the human intellectual Scale, you wil]
find that the leading nations of Europe started to wipe out
illiteracy long before we did, and have lower illiteracy rates
than we. Free compulsory elementary education was established
in several European countries before we achieved this goal. It
is today cheaper in time and money to become a professional
person in many European countries than in America. The impressive
figures on school attendance of American teenagers lose their
impressiveness when we compare the time actually spent in
classroom studies here and abroad--theirs being 1/4 to 1/3
longer each year than ours; hence our longer schooling is merely
a sort of stretch-out. We have nothing scholastically comparabie

to the European academic secondary school which is either free

26

or charges small fees tailored to the income of parents. Our
youngsters have to get this kind of liberal education in
college, losing precious time and having to spend more money
to boot--even in the state universities, where incidental and
boarding expenses exceed the cost of the secondary day schools
of Europe.

Many of our school problems spring from erroneous conceptions
of democracy and equality; from our unwillingness to recognize
the vast differences in mental endowment and determination which
are to be found in any representative group of children. We
must not make shibboleths of "democracy" and "equality."
National ideals are not realized in a set way which remains
unchanged forever. Democracy is a growing force. It never
reaches perfection. It ever finds new objectives. To achieve
it in a small, homogeneous farming community is one thing. Where
land is abundant and a man's success depends primarily on his
physical strength and courage, inequalities of mental endowment
are not very important. To maintain democracy in a highly
industrialized urban society is far more difficult, for here
man must use his mind more, and hence mental inequalities become
manifest. We cannot eliminate mental inequalities by running
our schools in a way which hides them, as when we admit students
regardless of their qualifications, or hand out diplomas and
degrees to everyone irrespective of scholastic achievement. We

pride ourselves on being democratic when we thus eliminate

27

competition and reward from the most important activity a
human being can engage in--brain work. But all we accomplish
by protecting the lazy and stupid child and neglecting the
talented is to impede technological progress.

This we could do with impunity so long as the choice was
left to us whether to advance, stand still, or progress. Today
we no longer have any choice at all. We are confronted with a
country whose leaders have openly declared that "they will bury
us;" a country that is larger and more populous than we; that
possesses all the necessary minerals for a high level technology,
many of which we must import; that is putting much greater
effort and proportionately more money into producing well-trained
scientists and engineers; and whose rate of industrial progress
is greater than ours. In this situation, ability to progress
technologically becomes an important element in relative military
strength.

Our present situation is such that survival as a free and
prosperous nation depends upon our being able to progress more
rapidly. This we shall not be able to do unless we bring
excellence into the schools; unless intellectual achievements
are properly rewarded so that competition will again come into
play. I doubt we can do this unless in some manner we set
educational standards. The only practical way at present seems
to be for American colleges to take on this responsibility.

They are in a position to pick and choose among applicants.

28

If they got together and agreed to keep increasing admissions
requirements year by year, they would gradually force higher
scholastic standards on our public schools. The private college
occupies a strategic position today. It is engaged in the
nation's most important activity--developing citizens who can
maintain our complex civilization and push it forward. Unless
we have such citizens and enough of them for our needs, our
country is in danger. For in truth and quite literally,
education is our first line of defense.

_ I should like to close with a well-lmown quotation from
Whitehead with which you must all be familiar but which is so
prophetically true that it cannot be repeated too often: "The

rule is absolute, the race which does not value trained

intelligence is doomed. Not all your victories on land or at
sea, can move back the finger of fate. Today we maintain
ourselves. Tomorrow science will have moved forward yet another
step, and there will be no appeal from the judgment which will

be pronounced on the uneducated."
