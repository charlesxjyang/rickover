Summary

In this segment of the 1971 AEC Authorizing Legislation Hearings, Admiral H.G. Rickover passionately advocates for the Light Water Breeder Reactor (LWBR), presenting it as the singular path to dramatically improve nuclear fuel utilization by converting thorium into fissionable uranium-233, thereby extending energy resources for "hundreds of years" compared to the mere 1-2% efficiency of current light water reactors. He stresses the LWBR's practical advantage, noting its adaptability to existing pressurized water reactor (PWR) plants, which limits the "technical uncertainties and extensive development inherent in other reactor concepts" and aligns with the utility industry's significant existing investment (estimated at $50 billion). While detailing fabrication progress, including $17 million committed to date for the Shippingport core (targeting 1974 operation with results by 1977), Rickover expresses skepticism about long-range cost projections, famously contrasting "academic" reactor designs with "practical" ones, highlighting the hidden complexities and true expenses of real-world development. He supports his arguments with detailed graphs on uranium mining requirements, cash flow expenditures, and cost comparisons (referencing WASH-1082 and Hanford data), and includes a letter from John W. Simpson of Westinghouse supporting the LWBR program's vigorous pursuit.

Admiral Ricxover. Thank you, Mr. Chairman.

The light water breeder reactor (LWBR) is expected to provide the capability to make a major improvement in the utilization of our nuclear fuel resources without. the many technical uncertainties and extensive development inherent in other reactor concepts, The LWBR is the only known approach to breeding in a light water thermal re- actor. If our development. program is successful, the LWBR core we are fabricating to install in the Shippingport reactor plant in 1974 will demonstrate technology which could permit the conversion of existing and future pressurized water reactors to self-sustaining breeders.

Once an LWBR type core is operating on the breeder cycle, making more uranium-233 from thorium during power operation than it con. sumes, it will be possible to convert about 50 percent of mined thorium into fissionable fuel for use in power production. The United States and world thorium reserves ‘ire So vast that this energy resource could provide mankind with an energy source for hundreds of years into the future.

By comparison, present types of light. water reactors, both boiling and pressurized types, can make available for power production only 1 percent to 2 percent of the potential energy in the nuclear-fuel ré- serves. This tremendous improvement in resource utilization can only be achieved through breeding.

Congress authorized the start of fabrication of the LWBR breeding- demonstration core in fiscal year 1969 and provided additional funds in. fiscal year 1970, The third increment of fabrication funds is $10 million of the $24 million requested for the LWBR in the Commis- sion’s fiscal year 1971 budeet request.

Manufacturing of the breeding demonstration core is progressing satisfactorily. This work is still on a schedule that should permit us to begin the breeding demonstration in 1974. I expect significant results from the breeding demonstration to be available beginning in 1977.

The LWBR is the only reactor system that promises significant ac ae pine in ‘fuel utilization while being devoid of many of the technical problems that must be solved to obtain high fuel utilization using other systems. In the long term, the LWBR may turn out to be the most practical approach to obtaining the high fuel utilization needed to make nuclear power fulfill its promise of providing man- kind an essentially unlimited energy source.

For many years the utility industry will be committed to basing the expanding nuclear central-station electrical-generating capacity on water-type reactors. Any manufacturer using a different coolant. will find it increasingly difficult to gain acceptance for his product since there will be a great reluctance to change to a new coolant when the water-cooled reactors will have such a wide base.

Ultimately, if events prove one of the alternate concepts is more desirable, the LWBR program will still have provided important contributions to general reactor technology—just as development done by Naval Reactors for the Shippingport project provided basic tech- nology on which the current application of nuclear power in industry is based. Since the expanding nuclear industry will for many years be based on water reactors, the technology developed in the LWBR pro- gram has an assured application.

This concludes my prepared statement on the LWBR, program. I would be happy to answer any questions you may have.

Admiral Rrcxover. Mr. Hosmer, as I have stated on many occasions in the past, I don’t believe comparisons of long-range cost projections of systems under development are meaningful. Even cost projections of systems already developed are of doubtful validity.

For example, you have seen direct evidence yourself of the wide variation in the costs phi for commercial nuclear central stations of existing types even during the past few years. Cost projections are based on assumptions about reactor performance, allowable heat flux, allowable fuel central temperature, reactor operating requirements, as well as economic assumptions.

The use of different assumptions often affects the calculated cost to a much greater extent than the differences one would calculate be- tween different systems. Using one set of assumptions, one system might look cheaper while, on a different set of assumptions, a different system might come out cheaper.

Further, the system predicted to have the lowest cost may not be technically achievable, no matter how much is spent on development.

I would like later on to read from an editorial I wrote about 15 years ago about the “academic” and the “practical” reactor.

Personally, I believe many of the assumptions made in comparative studies of the economics of future reactors can lead to false or im- proper conclusions.

One such assumption is ignoring the inflationary trend this coun- try has been experiencing during the past decade. If a reasonable rate of inflation is counted, this increases future costs and increases their impact on any cost studies using the so-called “present worth” method of discounting.

Another assumption usually made is that interest must be charged against the value of all owned assets.

In essence, these two assumptions say that you cannot afford to buy something of value now when prices are low and hold it for future use when prices will be higher.

Another assumption normally made is the size of the industry producing the product. The larger the industry, the cheaper the unit price of the product, so it is said. If you assume a large enough fuel- fabrication and processing industry, the fabrication and processing costs, of course, then become a very small part of fuel-cycle costs.

We did one study last year in which we estimated the fuel cost as- sociated with the fuel inventory and burnup parts of the fuel cycle for a 1,000-megawatt electrical, pressurized-water reactor of the type projected by industry as compared to a rough estimate we made of a 1,000-megawatt light-water breeder. We took into account the fact that after about 10 years of operation, the breeder would be on a self- sustaining cycle, where thorium is the only makeup fuel required, while it is necessary to continue mining uranium for the PWR through- out its life.

Also, once a given capacity of LWBR plants is operating on a breed- ing cycle, that capacity is available essentially forever without re- quiring the mining of more uranium.

Testimony. , ; practical versus academic reactors ;

Many years ago one was never allowed to'say anything about these theoretical reactors. So I hit on a device of publishing an editorial in the “Journal of Reactor Science and Technology,” volume. III, No. 3, which was a very secret document. And it sneaked by because no one knew I was publishing this editorial. Furthermore, it was'in a secret document.

I let this be known to people. So after it was published, they went to the Commission and said, “Look, there is no restricted data in this editorial.” So finally it was published. '

I would like to read this editorial because what I said many years ago, in my opinion, is still applicable today.

Important decisions relative to the future development of atomic power must frequently be made by people who do not necessarily have an intimate knowl- edge of the technical aspects of reactors. These people are, nonetheless, interested in what a reactor plant will do, how much it will cost, how long it will take to build, and how long and how well it will operate. :

You see, these are the same questions you are asking me, Mr. Hosmer, right now, the same thing. '

When they attempt to learn these things, they become aware of confusion exist- ing in the reactor business. There appears to be unresolved conflict on almost every issue that arises.

I believe that this confusion stems from a failure to distinguish between the academic and the practical. These apparent conflicts can usually be explained only when the various aspects of the issue are resolved into their academic anq practical components, To aid in this resolution, it is possible to define in a gen eral way those characteristics which distinguish the one from the other.

An academic reactor or reactor plant almost always has the following basic characteristics :

' (1) It is simple. (2) It is small. . (3) Itis cheap. : (4) Itislight. .

(5) It can be built very'quickly..- - - - .°. :

(6) It is very flexible in purpose. (‘omnibus reactor”). -

(7) Very little development is required. It will use mostly “off-the-shelf” components, —~ — Tt oste ss nn

(8) The reactor is in the study phase. It is not being built now.

On the other hand, a practical reactor plant can be distinguished by the following characteristics.

(1) It is being built now.

(2) It is behind schedule.

(3) It is requiring an immense amount of development on apparently trivial items. Corrosion, in particular, is a problem.

(4) It is very expensive.

(5) It takes a long time to build because of the engineering-development problems. :

(6) Itis large.

(7) It is heavy.

(8) It is complicated.

A common example can be given to indicate the application of the above generalities.

A fairly conventional academic power reactor might use natural or slightly enriched uranium rods in which the burnup is a minimum of 10,000 megawatt- days per ton. The fission products are confined to the fuel element by a simple cladding technique. The elements operate in high-pressure water at 600 degrees Fahrenheit.

In the practical reactor, difficulties are encountered. No element of the above type has been tested beyond a few thousand megawatt-days per ton. Hight years of work on high-uranium fuels have failed to produce cladding techniques which give really satisfactory performance in water at even 200 degrees Fahrenheit.

That was at that time.

At 600 degrees Fahrenheit, uranium reacts violently when exposed to water. The Chalk River experience shows the difficulty of maintaining a plant in which some fission products have escaped.

The tools of the academic-reactor designer are a piece of paper and a pencil with an eraser. If a mistake is made, it can always be erased and changed. If the practical reactor designer errs, he wears the mistake around his neck; it cannot be erased. Everyone can see it.

The academic reactor designer is a dilettante. He has not yet had to assume any real responsibility in connection with his projects. He is free to luxuriate in elegant ideas, the practical shortcomings of which can be relegated to the cate- gory of “mere technical details.” The practical reactor designer must live with these same technical details. Although recalcitrant and awkward, they must Be solved and cannot be put off until tomorrow. Their solutions require man- power, time, and money.

Unfortunately for those who must make far-reaching decisions without the benefit of an intimate knowledge of reactor technology, and unfortunately for the interested public, it is much easier to get the academic side of an issue than the practical side.

_ For example, the siting of reactor plants—I am sure this is the same issue you face in this committee on other areas.

For a large part those involved with the academic reactors have more in- clination and time to present their ideas in reports and orally to those who will listen. Since they are innocently unaware of the real but hidden difficulties of their plans, they speak with great facility and confidence. Those involved with practical reactors, humbled by their experience, speak less and worry more.

Yet it is incumbent on those in high places to make wise decisions, and it is reasonable and important that the public be correctly informed. It is con- sequently incumbent on all of us to state the facts as forthrightly as possible. Although it is probably impossible to have reactor ideas labeled as “practical” or “academic” by the authors, it is worthwhile for both the authors and the audience to bear in mind this distinction and to be guided thereby.

What is the present status of the fabrication of the LWBR breeding demonstration ?

Admiral Rickover. The new equipment that is required consists principally of the core, pressure-vessel head, and control-drive mech- anisms, as I said. Everything else already in the plant is being reused. Work on the new equipment began in fiscal year 1969, and the present status is as follows.

To date a total of a little over $17 million has been spent or com- mitted for fabrication of the breeding-demonstration core.

The order for the pressure-vessel head was placed in March 1969. The heavy forging has been completed, and fabrication is proceeding. Delivery is scheduled for mid-1972.

A development control drive mechanism has been fabricated and is on test. The design of the production mechanisms is complete. In arallel, lead units of this production design are being manufactured.

The core barrel was ordered in December 1968. Material has been

elivered and fabrication is proceeding. Delivery is scheduled for the

summer of 1971. Other structural parts of the core are also in manufacture.

The thorium-fuel pellets were ordered in February 1969. Work under this order continues.

The fabrication of the zircaloy tubing has started. The order for manufacture to the final sizes will be placed this fiscal year.

The remaining core fabrication work, including thorium dioxide— uranium-233 oxide binary pellets, zircaloy structures, fuel-rod sup- port, and fuel-rod assembly, and core assembly, is being done at Bettis. Manufacturing preparations at Bettis for this work started in early 1969. Some portions are already complete, and manufacturing devel- opment and preproduction are underway in all areas.

Work is on a schedule that permits starting the breeding demonstra- tion in 1974.

So, to sum it up, sir; this is all under manufacture. On all parts of the project the manufacturing is underway to fit the time schedule of 1974.

WESTINGHOUSE ELECTRIC CORPORATION, Pittsburgh, Pa., October 16, 1969. Dr. GLENN T. SEABORG, Chairman, U.8. Atomic Energy Commission, Washington, D.C.

Dear Dr. Seasore: I appreciate the opportunity I had to review the progress and status of the Light Water Breeder Reactor ( LWBR) program at the Bettis Atomic Power Laboratory on the occasion of the recent visit by the General Advisory Committee. The high quality of the work in progress was evident to me during our tours. The work accomplished to date strongly supports breeding in a light water-cooled, thorium-fueled reactor. The technology being developed is also of benefit to other reactor programs, particularly in the areas of reactor fuels and reactor materials.

Although Westinghouse is committed to the long range goal of developing an economical liquid metal fast breeder reactor ( LMFBR), it is my belief that the LWBR may well occupy an important place in commercial power in the event that the fast breeder program is delayed. Since the LWBR is less develonmental than other reactor concepts and can be backfitted into present day PWRs, the possibility of its early introduction by utilities, if conditions warrant, is evident. Thus, LWBR provides an excellent opportunity for the continued use of our light water reactor power generating facilities and operating experience.

We predict that the plutonium availability from light water reactors will exceed the demand for use in fast breeder reactors, emphasizing the need for more effort on plutonium recycle. At the GAC review, Bettis stated that pre- liminary studies indicate plutonium could be effectively used as a fissile fuel in pre-breeder operation to produce U233 for breeding. Thus, it appears that LWBR could serve a complementary role with the PWRs for the use of this material. '

Based on the ‘above, I feel that the LWBR breeder demonstration progre should be pursued vigorously by the Commission.

Sincerely, JOHN W. Simpson.
