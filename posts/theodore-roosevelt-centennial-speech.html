Summary

In this address, delivered on October 27, 1958, Rear Admiral H. G. Rickover pays tribute to Theodore Roosevelt on the occasion of his 100th birthday, emphasizing Roosevelt's enduring relevance as a "Modern American." Rickover highlights Roosevelt's self-made character, forged through deliberate effort, his broad intellectual curiosity spanning sciences and humanities (as referenced in the *Theodore Roosevelt Cyclopedia*), and his balanced moral compass. The speech details Roosevelt's foresight and leadership in national defense, including his pivotal role in modernizing the Navy (e.g., converting from coal to oil, improving gunnery through Lieutenant Sims) and his early recognition of aviation's military potential in 1898, drawing parallels to modern advancements like the nuclear submarines *NAUTILUS*, *SKATE*, and *SEAWOLF*. Rickover also stresses Roosevelt's pioneering advocacy for natural resource conservation and his concept of citizenship rooted in duties over rights, strongly condemning "hyphenated Americans" who prioritize group interests over national unity. The Admiral concludes by urging listeners to embody Roosevelt's principles of courageous effort, national service, and a focus on essential values over material gain or self-absorption, echoing Elihu Root's wish for Roosevelt's presence in their own time.

Let me first express my deep-felt appreciation and gratitude for the honor you have bestowed upon me by your award of the Roosevelt Distinguished Service Medal. It is a great pleasure to be with you tonight and join you in paying tribute to Theodore Roosevelt - a great man and a greatly beloved American. This solemn occasion gives us an opportunity to stop a moment in our daily rush from one activity to another and to assess our personal and national objectives, ideals, and actions in the light of the objectives, ideals, and actions of this illustrious American. Homage by oratory is an insubstantial thing unless, in the words of Roosevelt himself, "we sincerely endeavor to apply to the problems of the present precisely the qualities which in other crises enabled the men of

He was in harmony and balance within himself, for his heart, mind, and body were all developed to their highest potential. Despite his excellent family background, Roosevelt was a self- made man. He achieved greatness not because he was born a genius or had other exceptional natural endowments but because he made himself great. Time and again he stressed that pleasant as it is to be born with superior attainments, only a few can ever be that fortunate. He was naturally proudest of his good health and his athletic prowess because these he had created virtually himself, having been, as he expressed it, a sickly and awkward boy who was nervous and not overly confident. Such behavior drew from him the strongest words of condemnation. He returns to this subject time and again. Although many prominent Americans were obsessed with fear of tyranny by the majority, Roosevelt scoffed at this bugaboo. He pointed out that the real tyranny was being exerted by organized minorities. Often the minority groups fought against necessary action by the government which would have benefited the nation though at the expense of certain group interests. He realized that unless we accepted the ideal of national unity wholeheartedly we could be torn apart by conflicting interests. In his day the most vocal groups were those based on national origin. Their attempts to dictate foreign policy were intolerable, and so his strongest censure was directed at these men of divided national loyalties. He scornfully called them "hyphenated Americans" and warned that there was no place for them in this country.

Today there are few hyphenated Americans of that kind. But we do have many organized groups who try to gain special advantage through pressure tactics. Their tyranny, though more subtle, causes greater damage to the nation. Often they force their own code of behavior on an unwilling majority and they are eminently successful in preventing criticism of group-approved actions.
