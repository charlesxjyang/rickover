Michael Pupin Medal

1958 Speech Source: USNA Archives Box 30
View Original PDF View Original TXT

Summary

In a speech delivered upon receiving the Michael Pupin Medal at Columbia University in October 1958, Admiral H.G. Rickover passionately champions Western education as the singular force behind human progress and the dynamism of Western civilization. He presents Michael Pupin's life as an exemplary immigrant success story, nurtured by a liberal education spanning the US, Cambridge, and Berlin, to underscore the "common heritage" shared between America and Europe. Rickover asserts that the West's higher living standards and scientific miracles, such as penicillin, stem from European inventions like formal education, public libraries, universities, and academic freedom. He sharply contrasts this with pre-revolutionary China's examination system, arguing it fostered "mental ossification" and stagnation, leading to a society incapable of adapting to population growth and ultimately resulting in the brutal pragmatism exemplified by Mao Zedong. Rickover refutes the idea that Western civilization is merely materialistic, highlighting its contributions to human well-being. He concludes by criticizing contemporary American educational trends, which he labels "life-adjustment, behavioral conditioning, and a leveling downwards," urging a return to a robust liberal arts curriculum (echoing "Delenda est life-adjustment conditioning" from Cato the Elder) that fosters independent thought and is aligned with the comprehensive educational ideals articulated by Theodore Roosevelt.

A hundred years ago this, month, Francis Joseph, Emperor of Austria and Hungary, acquired still another rebellious Serbian sub- ject. Fifteen years later, this subject had enough of the stifling air of stagnation which pervades dying societies. A chance acquaint- ance with two traveling Americans had convinced the young man that in the New World he would find an invigorating atmosphere of freedom.

So he sold his school books, his sheepskin coat and cap, and sailed across the seas. Up to a point, Pupin's was the typical "poor | immigrant makes good in America" success story. He arrived here, a

But Pupin was a little unusual in that he had come here to continue his education. From the first day he set himself to master English and prepare for college. Atter, five years of hard physical work and night-time study, he passed the entrance examinations with honors and received a scholarship to Columbia College. No mean achievement this, when you consider the difficulties he had to overcome: Lack of funds, the need to master a new language, adjustment to mores as different from those of rural Banat on the Austrian frontier as one could imagine!

By tutoring other students and with the help of scholarships, Pupin was able to continue graduate study in mathematics and physics. He was advised that the best place for such study were the famous universities of Europe. He therefore returned to Europe and studied first at Cambridge, then at Berlin where he received his doctoral degree. Upon his return to America, he joined the faculty of Columbia University where he remained for the rest of his active academic life. He beseme one of America's great physicists and in- ventors. His primary field of interest was electrical communications. One of his great inventions brought about a reduction of attenuation and distortion in long-distance telephone communications and commem- orizes his name. In Europe, telepone lines using his invention are

In the making of this great inventor we can observe a happy

Begun in a Serbian village school, continued in secondary schools in the Voyvodins and in Bohemian Prague, and thereafter in an American college, com- pleted at the universities of Cambridge in England and Berlin in Germany - Pupin's education symbolizes the basic unity of academic life in all countries of Western civilization. This unity is a precious thing which cannot, to my mind, be overemphasized. The centennial of his birth offers an opportunity to reaffirm: the common heritage we share with Europe, whence have come the ancestors of nearly all living Americans, The spiritual and intellectual ties which bind the New World to the Old have been woven by millions of immigrants - not many of them Pupins, it is true, but all carrying with them across the seas something, however minuscule, which has gone into the making of America. Without our common intellectual heritage, and the unity of our basic outlook on life, there would be no substance in the joining together of our country with Europe by the North Atlantic Pact. There would be little assurance that this alliance will be firm enough to resist aggression through the iron curtain which divides two diametri- cally opposed civilizations. In a pamphlet issued in 1949, our State Department says of the Pact that it "has its roots in the common heritage and civilization of the peoples living on both shores of the North Atlantic Ocean."

To evaluate the precious heritage we possess in Western educa- tional theories and practices, we can do no better than to observe the results of another great system of training the young, which in its own sphere affected the quality of the life of a great civiliza~ tion quite as deeply as did Western education. I am referring to the examination system of pre-revolutionary China which for many centuries molded the mind of the Chinese people. It would be difficult to finda educational objectives so completely at odds with the basic goals of Western education than those evolved in Old China.

The famed examination system through which China's public officials were chosen from the most intelligent young men of the country was not "education" as the term has always been understood in the West. The system of examination through which a young man had to pass, tested not his ability to think clearly and independently, but his physical stamina and the capacity to reinterpret and re- phrase elegantly the words of wisdom contained in the "Four Books" and the "Five Classics", China's sacred literature. It was in effect a gigantic conditioning process which molded China's future leaders into exact copies of an ideal - an ideal set down once and for all by Confucious and his disciples in the fifth century B. C. It was the most elaborate machinery ever devised by man to perpetuate the status quo and to insure that the country's leaders would think exactly

alike and never swerve from the path laid down by the sages of the

past. The men who emerged victoriously from the rigors of the

examination cells could be relied upon to add not one lota to the nation's moral, esthetic, and political doctrines; they could be counted upon never to utter an original thought or to try to alter the ordering of the Celestial Empire which was considered perfect, hence in no need of improvement and eo ipso superior to all other civilizations of the world.

Even if disturbing influences from the out- side world are barred through the mightiest physical or mental Chinese wall, internal developments will gradually alter the clreum- — stances of life. By training, men's minds will have become accustomed to think in established grooves, they will lack the inventiveness and initiative necessary to meet the requirements of the new situation. Failure to readjust to changed circumstances will inevitably result in national decline; in a steady downward leveling of the quality of national life.

. This is what happened in China when outside influences and domestic changes destroyed the foundations of the "perfect ordering" of Chinese society. It is the fashion today among leaders of newly emancipated

countries to castigate Western civilization as materialistic. In- terestingly enough, it is the materialistic achievements of the West which the leaders and people of these countries crave and which they accept unhesitatingly, though seldom gratefully, from the hands of the West. They do not wish to acknowledge the source of the West's ability to provide large populations with the comforts and pleasures of civilized life. To my mind, all the philosophy, all the art, all the esthetics of other great civilizations are as nothing compared to the contribution made to the sum total of human well-being and happiness by just one small product of Western educa- tion - the single dose of penicillin which cures just one of the pain-~ ful diseases that is prevalent among backward people, but unknown in the materialist West - yaws. Nor can I see evidence of materialism in a civilization which produces dedicated men who will go out into countries where life is less safe and pleasant than in their home countries; who will go about in hot and unhealthy and uncomfortable places and freely dispense these doses of penicillin to all who need them. .

But we who have long benefited from the work done by such minds, must not, we dare not fall into the error of believing that we can retain the fruits of Western education without providing our children with such an education. I have long been deeply concerned with the danger to our future progress, and to the maintenance of our power- ful position in the world, which could easily result from present-~ day trends in American education. Our common heritage of Western educational ideals and techniques is not in high esteem among today's leaders in American public education. These leaders have already succeeded alarmingly in their effort to detour American education from the broad highway of Western education and into the dead-end of life-adjustment, behavioral conditioning, and a leveling downwards of curricula, students and teachers - all in the name of educational democracy. There are many ways to put a brake on technological pro- gress, but none so sure a5 producing young people who have not learned to use their minds effectively. Substituting vocational training, lessons in deportment and courses of preparation for leisure time activities for the time-tested liberal arts course developed by the West over two and a half millennia can easily do it.

A great American whose centennial celebration coincides with that of Pupin, Theodore Roosevelt, once summed up what a liberal education is and what it is good for: He said "A cultural education must include the classics. With these...every liberally educated man Should be familiar. He should also be familiar with at least one of the great modern culture languages...also...with a wide sweep of general history and pre-history, for a liberal scholar should certainly have clearly in mind the tremendous drama of man's pro- gress through the ages. A competent knowledge of science must also be part of any really liberal education, so that...the primary facts of the universe in which we live are vivid in his mind and form an integral portion of his stock of knowledge. The man with such broad liberal training is perhaps not apt to be a technical expert in any

special vocation; for his training stands outside the most direct
